# BRAIN CAN CHANGE

## LET'S APPLY FIRST PRINCIPLES REASONING

The world is very noisy. Everyone is shouting, "This is how it is!" But you can be quiet, and start with your own eyes. What do you see?

**You can start to see it with first principles reasoning.**

But what is first principles reasoning?

First principles thinking is a way of reasoning that begins with what is fundamentally true. Instead of relying on assumptions or accepted beliefs, it removes assumptions of accepted beliefs. And instead uses those verified truths  to build a clear understanding.

It is the process of stripping away assumptions,questioning the default, and analyzing the familiar. It does not accept something as true based only on popularity or authority, but instead aims for a clear understanding of your own.

**Why does this matter?**

Most people inherit their beliefs from others, such as parents, school, culture, or social media. They rarely question where those beliefs come from. So when one belief falls apart, they would have no structure to rely on. And without a solid foundation, it’s difficult to rebuild and think independently.

First principles reasoning puts you in the position of building your own understanding.

**How to do it:**

1. Identify what is genuinely known.
2. Remove assumptions and secondhand ideas.
3. Derive understanding from those fundamentals.
4. Check whether your reasoning is sound.

**Example:**

Someone says **"You need a career with high status to be happy."**

First principles approach:

What is happiness?

> It describes an internal state of well-being that varies based on individual values and perceptions

Does status guarantee contentment?

> There is no consistent evidence that external recognition directly leads to inner satisfaction and personal fulfillment. Status may influence perception, but it does not reliably produce lasting contentment.

Do I personally value status?

> This requires examining whether status genuinely matters to your personal values or if it has been socially conditioned.

What genuinely contributes to my happiness?

> This involves identifying what consistently results in your genuine satisfaction.

Hence, **the original statement might not apply to me, which captures first principles reasoning in a nutshell.**


## LET'S OBSERVE THAT THE BODY IS AN ADAPTIVE SYSTEM

Start by paying attention to your own body, you can begin to understand how it functions. 

The body isn’t governed by strict mechanical rules, it is fluid, responsive, and constantly adapting. Say we look at digestion speed. Variations in digestion from one day to the next are the body's responses to differences in nutrition, stress, and other factors, and are an example of this flexibility. Occasional muscle cramps can also happen as the body responds to stress from daily activities, this shows the processes of continuous repair.

We can look at a few more examples below to see how this pattern continues.

**Muscles:**

When you use your muscles, they grow stronger over time, but when you stop using them, they gradually weaken.  This shows that muscles respond directly to how they are used. Hence, regular use tends to lead to improved functional capacity, while lack of use often results in its decline.

**Metabolism:**

Metabolism refers to the collection of processes by which the body processes and uses energy. Energy is used to power everything from basic physiological functions to physical exertion and recovery. 

And because the body’s needs are always changing, metabolism is constantly adapting to what the body is responding to. If you eat less, your body tries to save energy. So energy levels may vary from day to day, influenced by nutrition, rest, and activity. These variations are not signs of dysfunction but instead a sign of a healthy system, because the better a system adapts, the more likely it is to improve chances of survival and stability.


**Immune System:**

When a virus enters the body, the immune system responds immediately. Fever may spike as a result of inflammation later on, which reflects the recovery of the immune cells that were activated during the infection phase. It is a sign that the system is responding as intended. Over time, the body recovers, and in many cases becomes more prepared for future exposure. This is because the immune system can retain a form of memory of the pathogen. 

**Cells:**

Cells in the body are constantly being created and destroyed. Every day, old cells die while new ones are formed. Skin replaces old cells with new ones all the time, the liver recovers from damage in ways most organs simply can't, and bones are constantly remodeling in response to stress and use. As a result, the body is always in a state of changes. While some cells and organs replace themselves in days, others in years, and some remain for a lifetime, studies suggest that the brain is one of the most adaptable organs in the human body, with changes occurring in milliseconds through the firing of neurons, and with adaptation and rewiring continuing to shift over the course of a lifetime.

Information box:

1. Skin cells, white blood cells, gut lining cells (layer of cells in your stomach and intestines), and platelets (part of the blood that function as natural clots): Days to weeks regeneration.
2. Red blood cells and liver cells: Months regeneration.
3. Fat cells, muscle cells, and bone cells: Years regeneration.
4. Neurons (brain cells) and heart muscle cells: Lifetime, they largely do not replace themselves, neurons (the primary cells of the brain responsible for transmitting information) and heart muscle cells (the cells that power the heart's continuous contractions) remain. But their connections can continue to change.

**When the pattern disappears:**

When this flexibility is lost, pathology often appears. For example, when muscle tissue becomes unable to respond properly to insulin, it can contribute to metabolic disorders such as diabetes. When the heart muscle loses its ability to contract, it may lead to conditions like cardiomyopathy. It’s the same with the immune system. When signaling becomes dysregulated, it can begin to misidentify the body’s own tissues, resulting in autoimmune disease.

In all of these cases, it can be shown that reduced adaptability tends to correlate with dysfunction. Losing the ability to adapt basically implies that the loss of adaptation is a contributing factor to disease development.

## LET'S EXPLORE THE FACT THAT THE BRAIN IS PART OF THE BODY

**The brain is an organ.** 

Just like the heart, the liver and the muscles. 
It's made of living cells, and those cells contains billions of neurons. Neurons can form connections with each other, and this is called interacting systems. 

Interacting systems is a structure where components continuously interact and react to each other. As we know from earlier, interacting systems that adapt are what make biological systems improve maintenance and function over time.

We can call the **sum of interacting systems** its own system an **adaptive system**, because of how they adapt over time.

Since neurons are biological cells, their connections are constantly modified by experience and activity. **Hence, at its core, the brain is fundamentally adaptable.**

**The brain is biological.**

And because the brain is biological like every other organ, it depends on metabolic needs (oxygen, glucose, energy, etc.). It is also influenced by multiple physiological factors, such as hormones, sleep, nutrition, stress, and time.

So there is no reason to assume it is the only organ that does not adapt.

But the brain is unique because it can also be influenced by psychological factors. The reason is that psychological processes such as thoughts and emotions are produced by brain activity.

So the brain isn't just influenced by physiological factors, it is also influenced by psychological ones. And since the brain is inherently influenced by multiple factors, it can be concluded that it is the sum of interacting systems that can change (adaptive system).

**Now we can view the brain as an adaptive system that can change, this means it can change, and this change is known as neuroplasticity.**

Neuroplasticity is part of neuroscience. Neuroscience is the study of the brain and nervous system.

**You don't need to see your brain adapting to know it does.**

You can't see your immune system learning a pathogen. You can't see your muscle fibers repairing after exercise. But you know those things happen because the body is an adaptive system. The brain is no different. It's just harder to observe directly.

**Evidence you can observe:**

- You learned language as a child. 
- You learned to walk. 
- You learned to read. 
- You're learning even right now. Every sentence you read might cause small changes in your brain.

You've been experiencing neuroplasticity your entire life, even if you aren't thinking of the word constantly.

**And the conclusion follows necessarily:**

If the body adapts, and the brain is part of the body, then the brain adapts.

Hence your brain can change. So what feels like a permanent emotion today may not last until tomorrow because the brain follows the same law as an adaptive system (biology).

## LET'S EXPLORE WHAT SYLLOGISM MEANS

**A syllogism is a logical structure:**

- Premise 1
- Premise 2
- Conclusion

If the premises are true, and the syllogism structure is sound, the conclusion is typically reliable.

**The classic example:**

- All humans are mortal. (Premise 1)
- Socrates is a human. (Premise 2)
- Therefore, Socrates is mortal. (Conclusion)

**The syllogism of this book:**

- All biological systems adapt continuously. (Premise 1)
- The brain is a biological system. (Premise 2)
- Therefore, the brain adapts continuously. (Conclusion)

**This book is a syllogism for your brain:**

1. The body adapts.
2. The brain is body.
3. The brain adapts.
